# Day 3 — Question

**Topic:** Training and post-training mechanics
**Subtopic:** Where the preference gradient actually lands — when chosen and rejected differ in only a handful of load-bearing tokens, does a length-normalized preference loss (SimPO) concentrate the update on the discriminative tokens or smear it across the whole sequence, and what does that mean for what the trained critic has *actually* learned about tone
**Asker:** Kidane Gebremedhin Gidey
**Date:** 2026-05-08

## The question

In Week 11 I trained a LoRA-adapted critic with **SimPO** on preference pairs drawn from short, format-constrained sales drafts. The chosen and rejected texts in each pair are almost the same body — same prospect framing, same opener shape, same length envelope — and the difference between them is concentrated in a small number of load-bearing tokens: a tone marker, a presumptuous verb, a closer that overclaims. The shipped critic scores chosen above rejected reliably on the dev partition. What I do not know is *what part of the sequence the model is actually using* to make that call. The preference loss is computed over the whole sequence, length-normalized, but the preference signal in the data lives in only a handful of tokens. Those are not the same thing.

The question I want my partner to research:

> **In a length-normalized preference optimizer like SimPO, how does the per-sequence loss decompose into per-token gradient contributions, and on preference pairs whose chosen and rejected texts are near-identical except for a few load-bearing tokens, does the update concentrate on those discriminative tokens — or does it smear across the shared body, training the critic on a global stylistic shift that merely correlates with the tone signal?**
>
> Three sub-questions, anchored to the critic I shipped:
>
> 1. **The decomposition.** SimPO's loss is the average log-probability of the sequence under the policy, run through a Bradley-Terry comparator. At the gradient level, how does that average decompose? Does each token contribute a per-token chosen-minus-rejected push, or does the length normalization make the gradient on a single token depend on every other token in the same sequence? What does that decomposition look like for a chosen and rejected that share most of their tokens and differ in a few?
>
> 2. **Where the signal lands.** On near-identical pairs, the shared tokens have nearly the same logprobs under chosen and rejected branches, so their per-token contributions roughly cancel. The discriminative tokens have asymmetric logprobs and so carry most of the *signal*, but length normalization spreads the *gradient* across the whole sequence. Which effect dominates in practice — does the trained adapter learn a sharp "tone-token detector," or a diffuse "this whole text feels off" gradient field that happens to fire when the discriminative tokens appear? What experiment, on a small open model with synthetic near-identical pairs, would distinguish those two outcomes?
>
> 3. **What the critic has actually learned.** If the gradient does smear, then the critic is not really scoring tone violations — it is scoring a global stylistic shift that correlates with where tone violations live in the training distribution. That is a different object, and it fails differently in deployment: it generalizes well to drafts that share the training distribution's *body shape* and fails on drafts whose body shape is unfamiliar even when the tone is fine. What in-training or post-training diagnostic — per-token gradient attribution on a held-out probe set, mask-the-discriminative-tokens-and-see-if-preference-survives, embedding-distance between chosen-rejected pairs vs the critic's reward gap — would tell me which kind of object I shipped?
>
> The punchline I actually need: **a way to tell, from inspecting the trained adapter and a small probe set rather than waiting for production failures, whether my critic is doing token-localized tone-violation detection or sequence-level stylistic-shift detection — because the second one is the kind of thing that passes a held-out eval and over-rejects on the first prospect whose body shape the training data did not cover.**

## Why this gap exists in my work

The shipped critic sits in front of the composer as a rejection-sampling layer. The deployment path is straightforward when the inference-time draft looks like a training draft: same prospect framing, same length envelope, same opener shape. What I cannot currently defend is what happens when the draft is structurally novel — a longer body because the prospect's signal needs more setup, an unusual opener because the prospect type is new, a different closer shape because we are testing a different call-to-action. If the critic learned *token-localized* tone detection, it should generalize to those cases as long as the tone tokens themselves are absent. If the critic learned *sequence-level stylistic shift*, it will mis-fire on novel body shapes whether or not their tone is fine — and the kill-switch downstream (which escalates to a human SDR after consecutive rejections) will start firing on perfectly good drafts.

The deeper reason I cannot defend this is that my evaluation surface looks like my training distribution. The dev partition is short, format-constrained, and shaped the same way as the train partition; the held-out partition is too. A critic that learned the *correlation* between body shape and tone outcome will look perfect on both. The failure mode reveals itself only on out-of-distribution body shapes, which are exactly the kinds of drafts the v0.2 expansion (threaded multi-turn traces with longer, more varied bodies) will introduce. I am about to expand the evaluation in a direction that, if my critic is the smearing kind, will look like a sudden inexplicable degradation — when the real story is that the critic was always the smearing kind and the old eval just hid it.

There is also a sharper, more local reason. The memo's tone-preservation paragraph already self-flags that the dimension is partly subjective and that explicit anti-marker phrases were patched in deterministically as a backstop. That backstop helps me at deployment time but it does not tell me what the LoRA-adapted critic itself has internalized. If the critic is doing global stylistic-shift detection, the deterministic anti-marker layer is doing more work than the model card credits it for, and the load-bearing claim that the SimPO training is the safety lift becomes harder to defend to a reviewer who pushes on it.

| **Resolvable in one explainer** | A 600–1,000 word explainer can write down the per-token decomposition of the SimPO loss, run a small open-model SimPO training on synthetic near-identical pairs, produce a per-token gradient attribution heatmap, and propose one cheap probe (mask the discriminative tokens, rescore, see if the preference flips) that runs in a few seconds on a held-out slice. |

## What a satisfying answer looks like

In one or two sentences: **(a)** the explainer derives, in plain notation, how a length-normalized preference loss redistributes a single sequence-level scalar into per-token gradients — and shows, on a worked toy example with a chosen and rejected that differ in one token, exactly how much of the update actually lands on that token versus the shared body, **(b)** it runs a small reproducible experiment on an open base + LoRA where the same preference loss is trained on synthetic near-identical pairs, and it presents a per-token attribution map showing whether the discriminative tokens dominate or whether the smear dominates, with the experimental setup written down so I can re-run it on my own pairs, and **(c)** it gives me one cheap diagnostic — a mask-the-discriminative-tokens probe or a per-token attribution scan — that I can run against the shipped adapter today, with the threshold or qualitative pattern that distinguishes "token-localized tone detector" from "sequence-level stylistic-shift detector."
